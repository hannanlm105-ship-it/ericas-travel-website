India

A land of ancient temples, spice markets, and breathtaking diversity

Things to see & do

Temples, palaces & endless wonder

India is a sensory overload in the best possible way. From the moment you land, colours, sounds, and aromas compete for your attention. The Taj Mahal in Agra is an obvious starting point — and despite the crowds, it will genuinely take your breath away at sunrise.

Head north to Rajasthan for palace-hopping through Jaipur, Jodhpur, and Udaipur. The blue streets of Jodhpur's old city and the lake-reflected palaces of Udaipur feel almost dreamlike. Further south, Kerala's backwaters offer a completely different pace — a houseboat drifting past rice paddies is one of travel's great pleasures.

For spiritual seekers, Varanasi on the banks of the Ganges is unlike anywhere else on earth — raw, overwhelming, and deeply moving. Don't miss the evening Ganga Aarti ceremony.

"India will always have something new to teach you — no matter how many times you return."
— Erica, on her third visit
How to get there

Getting to India

India is well connected internationally, with major airports in Delhi (Indira Gandhi International), Mumbai (Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj), Bangalore, and Chennai. Most long-haul flights route through Delhi or Mumbai.

Most nationalities require a visa — the e-Visa system makes this straightforward, typically taking 2–4 business days to process online. Indian trains are an experience in themselves and a fantastic way to travel between cities once you arrive. Book train tickets early through the IRCTC website.

Best time to visit
October – March is ideal for most of India — cool, dry weather perfect for sightseeing. Avoid April–June (extreme heat) and July–September (monsoon) unless you specifically want to experience the rains. Rajasthan is best November–February.

Taj Mahal at sunrise — arrive before 7am to beat the crowds

Southeast Asia

Vietnam

Limestone karsts, lantern-lit streets, and a cuisine that will haunt you

Things to see & do

From Hanoi to Hội An

Vietnam stretches over 1,600km from north to south, and every region has its own distinct character. Start in Hanoi — the capital's chaotic Old Quarter is best explored on foot, dodging scooters and discovering hidden coffee shops behind wooden shopfronts.

Ha Long Bay is the image most people associate with Vietnam, and rightly so — over 1,600 limestone islands rising from emerald waters. Take an overnight cruise to truly appreciate the scale. South of Hanoi, the town of Hội An is one of Southeast Asia's most beautifully preserved trading ports. Its famous lantern-lit streets glow every evening, and the tailors can make you a custom outfit in 24 hours.

Ho Chi Minh City (Saigon) in the south offers a faster pace, excellent street food, and fascinating war history at the Cu Chi Tunnels and War Remnants Museum.

"I came for Ha Long Bay. I stayed for the bánh mì at 7am on a Hội An street corner."
— Erica, Hội An
How to get there

Getting to Vietnam

Vietnam has three main international airports: Nội Bài (Hanoi), Đà Nẵng (central), and Tân Sơn Nhất (Ho Chi Minh City). Most travellers fly into Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City. Airlines including Vietnam Airlines, VietJet, and international carriers connect major Asian hubs.

Most nationalities can get a visa on arrival or an e-visa. The e-visa is processed online within 3 business days and is valid for 90 days with single or multiple entry. Within Vietnam, budget flights, overnight trains, and sleeper buses are all affordable options.

Best time to visit
February – April for central and north Vietnam — mild and dry. November – January is best for the south. The climate varies greatly by region, so timing depends on where you're headed. Hội An can flood October–November.

Himalayan Kingdom

Bhutan

The last Shangri-La — where happiness is measured, not just felt

Things to see & do

The kingdom that measures happiness

Bhutan is unlike anywhere else in Asia. This tiny Himalayan kingdom has deliberately chosen to limit tourism in order to protect its culture and environment — which means every visitor gets a pristine, unhurried experience.

The must-do is the Paro Taktsang hike, better known as Tiger's Nest. Clinging to a 3,000m cliff face, this sacred Buddhist monastery takes about two hours to reach on foot — but the view from across the valley is one of travel's great rewards. The capital Thimphu offers a fascinating blend of ancient tradition and modern life, all without a single traffic light.

The Punakha Dzong — a fortress-monastery at the confluence of two rivers — is arguably the most beautiful building in the Himalayas. Visit in spring when the jacaranda trees bloom purple around it.

"Tiger's Nest will humble you. The hike, the monastery, the silence — all of it."
— Erica, Paro Valley
How to get there

Getting to Bhutan

Bhutan is only accessible by air through Druk Air or Bhutan Airlines. Flights connect from Bangkok, Delhi, Kathmandu, Singapore, and a handful of other regional hubs to Paro International Airport — one of the world's most dramatic landings.

All tourists must pay a Sustainable Development Fee of USD 100 per person per night, which covers accommodation, meals, transport, and a guide. This is not optional — but it ensures tourism remains high-quality and low-impact. Visas are processed through your licensed tour operator.

Best time to visit
March – May for spring blooms and clear skies. September – November for post-monsoon clarity with incredible mountain views. Winter (December–February) is cold but crowd-free and magical.
Erica's top picks
Hike to Tiger's Nest — start at 7am to beat the heat
Punakha Dzong in spring — the purple jacaranda is spectacular
Attend a Tsechu festival if your dates align
Try ema datshi — Bhutan's famous chilli cheese dish
Quick facts
Currency: Bhutanese Ngultrum (BTN)
Language: Dzongkha
Visa: Via licensed tour operator only

Southeast Asia

Thailand

Golden temples, turquoise islands, and the world's greatest street food

Things to see & do

Temples, islands & night markets

Thailand is a destination that rewards every type of traveller. Bangkok is one of Asia's great cities — chaotic, delicious, and utterly absorbing. The Grand Palace and Wat Pho are unmissable; equally essential is losing yourself in a Chinatown street market at midnight with a bowl of noodle soup.

The north offers a calmer experience. Chiang Mai is the cultural heart of the country — from the 300 temples within the old city walls to the night bazaars and cooking classes, it's a city you could happily spend two weeks in. The hill tribe villages in the surrounding mountains offer insight into a way of life that has remained largely unchanged for centuries.

And then there are the islands. The Andaman coast (Krabi, Koh Lanta) and the Gulf of Thailand (Koh Samui, Koh Tao) offer different experiences — the Andaman wins for dramatic limestone scenery, while the Gulf is better for scuba diving.

"Bangkok's street food at 1am, eaten on a plastic stool by a canal — that's when I fell completely in love."
— Erica, Bangkok
How to get there

Getting to Thailand

Suvarnabhumi Airport (Bangkok) is one of Southeast Asia's busiest hubs and connects to virtually every major city in the world. Don Don Mueang is Bangkok's budget airline terminal. Phuket and Chiang Mai also have international airports.

Most nationalities receive a 30-day visa exemption on arrival, extendable once at a local immigration office. Thailand is well served by budget airlines — AirAsia, Nok Air, Lion Air — making domestic travel fast and affordable.

Best time to visit
November – April is the dry season — ideal nationwide. The Andaman coast is best avoided May–October (monsoon). The Gulf coast has its own schedule — Koh Samui is fine October–April.

Southeast Asia

Malaysia

Rainforest, skyscrapers, and a food culture that defines the nation

Things to see & do

From KL's skyline to Borneo's jungle

Malaysia is one of Southeast Asia's most underrated destinations — a country of extraordinary contrasts where colonial architecture sits alongside Islamic skyscrapers, and ancient rainforest lies within an hour of the capital.

Kuala Lumpur is the obvious starting point. The Petronas Twin Towers are genuinely impressive in person, but the city's real soul is found in its hawker stalls — Jalan Alor night market, the coffee shops of Chinatown, and the roti canai eaten at a mamak stall at midnight.

George Town in Penang is a UNESCO World Heritage city and arguably the food capital of Asia. The street art, heritage shophouses, and multi-cultural food scene could occupy you for a full week. For nature lovers, the ancient rainforests of Taman Negara and the orangutan sanctuaries of Borneo (Sabah and Sarawak) are world-class wildlife experiences.

"I went to Malaysia for the Petronas Towers. I stayed for the char kway teow in Penang."
— Erica, George Town
How to get there

Getting to Malaysia

Kuala Lumpur International Airport (KLIA) is a major regional hub with direct flights from Europe, the Middle East, Australia, and Asia. The budget terminal KLIA2 serves AirAsia and other low-cost carriers — useful for onward travel within Southeast Asia.

Most nationalities do not need a visa for Malaysia (90-day entry on arrival). The country is well connected internally by budget airlines, buses, and trains. The KTM rail network connects KL to the Thai border, and flights to Borneo are frequent and affordable.

Best time to visit
March – October for the west coast (KL, Penang, Langkawi). October – February for the east coast and Borneo. Malaysia is tropical year-round — some rain is always possible but rarely ruins a trip.

Penang's Gurney Drive hawker centre at sunset
Sunrise at Batu Caves — arrive before the crowds
Orangutan sanctuary in Sepilok, Sabah
